## When to Use

Use this when you're:
- Hearing the same objections repeatedly and not closing
- Training a sales team on objection handling
- Trying to figure out why qualified prospects don't buy
- Getting "I need to think about it" and not knowing how to respond
- Wondering if you should lower your price (you almost certainly shouldn't)

## The Framework

### The Four Types

Every objection, no matter how it's worded, maps to one of four root causes:

| Objection Type | What They Say | What They Mean | Root Cause |
|---------------|-------------|----------------|------------|
| **Price** | "It's too expensive" / "I can't afford it" | "I don't see enough value to justify this" | Value perception gap |
| **Time** | "I need to think about it" / "Not right now" | "I'm not convinced enough to decide" | Insufficient urgency or conviction |
| **Authority** | "I need to talk to my spouse/partner" | "I can't decide alone" or "I'm stalling" | Decision-making constraint or avoidance |
| **Fit** | "I'm not sure this is for me" / "I've tried this before" | "I don't believe this will work for MY situation" | Perceived likelihood problem |

### The Universal Response Pattern

All four objections use the same response structure:

1. **Acknowledge** — Validate their concern. Don't dismiss it. "I totally understand."
2. **Isolate** — Confirm this is the ONLY concern. "If we could address that, would you be ready to move forward?"
3. **Address** — Tackle the underlying belief, not the surface statement.
4. **Re-ask** — After addressing, return to the close.

### Objection 1: Price

**Surface:** "That's more than I expected" / "I can't afford it" / "Can you do a discount?"

**Underlying belief:** The perceived value is less than the price. This is ALWAYS a value problem, never a price problem.

**Response approach:**
- **Isolate:** "If money weren't a factor, would you want to do this?"
  - If YES → It's genuinely price. Address with payment plans, ROI breakdown, cost-of-inaction.
  - If NO → It's not about price. Ask "What else is holding you back?" (It's probably Fit or Time.)
- **Reframe the cost:** "What's it costing you right now to NOT solve this? You mentioned you've already spent [$X from the Overview step]."
- **Break it down:** "That works out to [$X/day]. Is [dream outcome] worth [$X/day] to you?"
- **Stack the value:** Walk through everything included. (See bonus-stacking-psychology.md)

Do NOT lower the price. If you lower the price, you confirm their belief that it wasn't worth the original price.

### Objection 2: Time

**Surface:** "I need to think about it" / "Let me sleep on it" / "Can I get back to you?"

**Underlying belief:** They are not convinced enough to decide now. If they were, they would decide now.

**Response approach:**
- **Surface the real concern:** "That makes total sense. What specifically do you want to think about?" (Whatever they say next is the REAL objection. Handle that.)
- **The 6-month reframe:** "I totally understand. Here's what I've noticed though: most people who say 'let me think about it' come back 6 months later wishing they'd started 6 months ago. Where would you be if you'd started 6 months ago?"
- **Reduce risk:** Offer guarantee, trial period, or reduced-commitment entry.

The key insight: "think about it" is almost never followed by a yes later. The emotional state that would lead to a yes exists on the call. Once they hang up, logic and fear take over.

### Objection 3: Authority

**Surface:** "I need to talk to my spouse" / "My business partner makes these decisions" / "I need to run this by my team"

**Underlying belief:** Either genuine (shared finances, shared decision-making) or a stall tactic.

**Response approach:**
- **Test for genuine vs. stall:** "If your [spouse/partner] said 'whatever you think is best,' would you do it?"
  - If YES → Genuine. Help them bring the partner in: "Would a quick summary help, or should we do a 10-minute call with them?"
  - If NO → Stall. "What concerns do you think they'd raise?" Then handle THOSE concerns.
- **Prevent it:** Ask about decision-making authority during the Clarify step, not after the pitch. "Is there anyone else involved in this decision?" If yes, include them from the start.

### Objection 4: Fit

**Surface:** "I'm not sure this is right for me" / "I've tried something like this before" / "My situation is different"

**Underlying belief:** They believe the result is possible for others but not for them. This is a perceived likelihood of achievement problem (Value Equation, denominator).

**Response approach:**
- **Get specific:** "What about your situation makes you unsure?" (Force them to name the doubt.)
- **Match to proof:** "We've worked with someone in almost exactly your situation — [name/description]. They got [specific result]."
- **Acknowledge uniqueness:** "You're right that [their unique factor]. That's actually why we built [specific feature] into the program."
- **Lower commitment:** "Would you be open to [smaller first step] to see if it's the right fit before committing fully?"

### The Reframing Method (Advanced)

From "After Closing 4000+ Sales," Hormozi introduces the 3A reframing framework:

> "Reframing is the one to three sentences that you say after a prospect says anything but yes that increases the likelihood that whatever your next thing you say gets them to buy."
> — Alex Hormozi, "After Closing 4000+ Sales, I Discovered a New Method to Close Deals Faster"

The reframe takes their objection and shifts the lens before you respond to it. Instead of arguing against their concern, you change how they see the concern.

## The Conviction Foundation

Behind all objection handling is the salesperson's conviction. If you don't believe in the product, the price, or yourself, no technique will save you.

> "There are nine things that brilliant sales people do differently."
> — Alex Hormozi, "Sales Was Hard Until I Understood These 9 Concepts"

Conviction is #1 on Hormozi's list. An unconvinced salesperson leaks doubt through tonality, word choice, and pacing. The prospect picks up on it unconsciously.

## Output

After reading this framework, you should be able to:
1. Classify any objection into one of the four types within seconds
2. Apply the Acknowledge → Isolate → Address → Re-ask pattern
3. Tell the difference between a genuine concern and a stall
4. Prevent objections by addressing them earlier in the sales conversation (CLOSER steps C and O)
