## When to Use

Use this when you need to:
- Name a new offer, program, or course
- Name a lead magnet that converts
- Name bonuses in your offer stack (unnamed bonuses lose perceived value)
- Rename an existing offer that sounds generic
- Create a naming system for a product line

Good names do the selling for you. Bad names require explanation. If you have to explain what your offer is after someone hears the name, the name failed.

## The Core Formula

Hormozi's naming methodology, taught primarily through his lead magnet framework, follows a three-part structure:

**[Result] + [Timeframe] + [Container]**

Each element serves a specific psychological function:

### Element 1: Result (What They Get)

The result should be the dream outcome expressed in the prospect's language, not yours. Concrete beats abstract. Specific beats vague.

**Weak results:** "Success," "Growth," "Transformation," "Mastery"
**Strong results:** "Revenue," "Six-Pack," "First 5 Clients," "20 Pounds Lost"

The result in the name should be the thing they'd tell a friend about: "I'm doing this thing that helps me get [result]." If [result] is vague, they can't explain it, which means they can't refer it.

### Element 2: Timeframe (How Fast)

The timeframe compresses perceived time delay — one of the denominator variables in the Value Equation. A timeframe in the name signals speed, which signals value.

**Timeframe options:**
- Days: "7-Day," "21-Day," "30-Day"
- Weeks: "6-Week," "12-Week"
- Minutes: "60-Second," "5-Minute," "90-Minute"
- Sessions: "First-Session," "One-Call"
- Unique time markers: "Before Breakfast," "Over Lunch," "Weekend"

The timeframe should feel aggressive but believable. "30-Day Revenue Sprint" works. "1-Day Business Empire" doesn't. The line is: could a reasonable person believe this if they saw the proof?

### Element 3: Container (The Format)

The container tells the prospect what they're getting — the delivery vehicle. It sets expectations for format, effort, and commitment level.

**Container words and what they signal:**

| Container | Signals | Effort Level | Best For |
|-----------|---------|-------------|----------|
| **Challenge** | Community, accountability, urgency | Medium-High | Group programs, launches |
| **Sprint** | Speed, intensity, short duration | High | Quick-result offers |
| **Blueprint** | Step-by-step, structured, comprehensive | Medium | Courses, guides |
| **System** | Automated, repeatable, professional | Low-Medium | Done-for-you, SaaS |
| **Accelerator** | Speed + support, premium | High | High-ticket programs |
| **Audit** | Diagnostic, personalized, specific | Low | Lead magnets, discovery calls |
| **Toolkit** | Practical, ready-to-use, tactical | Low | Resource bundles |
| **Playbook** | Strategic, sequenced, actionable | Medium | Guides, courses |
| **Masterclass** | Expert-led, educational, premium | Medium | Workshops, training |
| **Protocol** | Scientific, precise, step-by-step | Medium | Health, fitness, technical |
| **Workshop** | Interactive, hands-on, live | Medium-High | Events, group sessions |
| **Vault** | Exclusive, curated, high-value collection | Low | Resource libraries |

## Beyond the Formula: Advanced Naming Principles

### The Differentiation Imperative

> "Marketing is about different. Business is about better."
> — Alex Hormozi, "How To Create A GRAND SLAM Offer"

Your name must sound different from every other offer in your space. If your market is full of "masterclasses," use "sprint." If everyone runs "challenges," run a "protocol." The container word is the easiest lever for differentiation.

**Test:** Google your proposed name. If the first page is full of similar names, change the container word.

### The Cocktail Party Test

Can someone who bought your offer describe it to a friend in one sentence using just the name?

- "I'm doing the 6-Week Shred Challenge" - PASSES (friend knows: result = shredded, timeframe = 6 weeks, format = challenge)
- "I'm in the Elevate program" - FAILS (friend knows nothing)

### The Proprietary Name Play

For your core offer (not lead magnets or bonuses), consider adding a proprietary element — a branded word or phrase that you own:

- "Grand Slam Offer" (Hormozi's own)
- "The CLOSER Framework" (Hormozi's sales method)

Proprietary names create intellectual property. They make your methodology unforgettable and unreplicable. The formula: [Your Branded Term] + [Container].

### Naming Bonuses

Bonuses MUST be named. An unnamed bonus is an afterthought. A named bonus is a product.

Compare:
- "You also get a meal plan" (unnamed = low perceived value)
- "You also get The Complete Meal Blueprint" (named = feels like a standalone product worth buying)

For bonuses, use the same Result + Timeframe + Container formula, but keep names shorter and punchier. Bonuses are supporting actors, not the lead.

### Naming for Different Price Points

| Price Point | Naming Style | Example |
|-------------|-------------|---------|
| Free (lead magnet) | Punchy, result-forward, compressed timeframe | "The 5-Minute Offer Audit" |
| Low-ticket ($47-$497) | Clear, specific, slightly longer | "The 30-Day Lead Gen Blueprint" |
| Mid-ticket ($997-$4,997) | Premium container, professional | "The Revenue Acceleration System" |
| High-ticket ($5,000+) | Proprietary, exclusive, minimal | "The Grand Slam Intensive" |

At higher price points, names become more restrained. Luxury signals through brevity, not description.

## Common Naming Mistakes

1. **Clever over clear:** "The Phoenix Protocol" sounds cool but communicates nothing. "The 90-Day Revenue Sprint" is less clever and 10x more effective.

2. **No result in the name:** "The Business Bootcamp" — what business result? "The First 10 Clients Bootcamp" — now you know.

3. **No timeframe:** "The Sales Mastery Program" — how long? "The 12-Week Sales Mastery Program" — now I can commit.

4. **Generic container:** "The Course" or "The Program" — meaningless. Pick a container that signals the experience.

5. **Too long:** "The Complete Step-By-Step Revenue Generation and Client Acquisition Accelerator for Service-Based Business Owners" — no one will say this out loud. 3-7 words maximum.
