## When to Use

Use this when you're:
- Starting a business and don't know where to begin getting customers
- Deciding between outreach vs. ads vs. content
- Running out of warm contacts and wondering if cold outreach makes sense
- Building outreach scripts for either warm or cold audiences
- Trying to get customers fast with zero budget

## The Framework

### The Core Decision

Warm and cold outreach solve different problems at different stages:

| Factor | Warm Outreach | Cold Outreach |
|--------|-------------|--------------|
| **Who** | People who know you | Strangers |
| **Trust level** | Pre-existing | Zero |
| **Conversion rate** | 5-25% | 0.5-3% |
| **Scalability** | Limited by network size | Unlimited |
| **Cost** | $0 | $0-500/mo for tools |
| **Best for** | First 5-20 customers | Scaling beyond network |
| **Speed to first sale** | Days to weeks | Weeks to months |

### When to Use Warm Outreach

**Always start here.** Hormozi's advice is unambiguous:

> "Content, you reach out to people, and you do that for four straight hours every day. You reach out to 10,000 people, you'll have the business you want."
> — Alex Hormozi, "I Helped Business Owners Overcome Their Fear of Failure"

Warm outreach is the fastest path to your first customers because:
1. Trust already exists (they know you)
2. You can ask for referrals (even if they're not the buyer)
3. Feedback is immediate (they'll tell you what's wrong with your offer)
4. It costs nothing

**Volume benchmark:**

> "You're 6 months away, seriously. You got to reach out to, let's call it, 10,000 people."
> — Alex Hormozi, "I Helped Business Owners Overcome Their Fear of Failure"

**The warm outreach approach:**
- Start with your phone contacts, social connections, past clients
- Use the ACA framework: Acknowledge relationship, Compliment genuinely, Ask who they know
- Don't pitch directly. Ask "do you know anyone who [ideal customer description]?"
- This removes pressure and often leads them to self-identify

### When to Use Cold Outreach

Switch to or add cold outreach when:
1. You've exhausted your warm network (everyone's been contacted at least once)
2. You have a proven offer (warm outreach validated it)
3. You need more volume than your network provides
4. Your target market is specific and findable (B2B, local businesses, creators)

Cold outreach requires more structure because there is no pre-existing trust:
- **Targeting must be precise** (wrong audience = zero response)
- **Messaging must prove relevance** (prove you know their world)
- **Volume must be higher** (compensate for lower conversion rate)
- **Follow-up must be systematic** (5+ touches before giving up)

### The Hybrid Approach

Most businesses should run BOTH once warm is working:

| Time Block | Activity |
|-----------|---------|
| Morning (2 hrs) | Cold outreach (volume play) |
| Midday (1 hr) | Content creation (nurtures both warm and cold leads) |
| Afternoon (1 hr) | Warm outreach + follow-ups |

### Common Mistakes

1. **Skipping warm to go straight to cold** - Lower conversion rate + no proof = wasted effort
2. **Low volume** - 10 outreaches/day is not enough. 100 is the minimum for meaningful data
3. **Pitching in the first message** - Always lead with value or a question, not a pitch
4. **No follow-up** - Most responses come on touch 2-5, not touch 1
5. **Generic messaging** - "I help businesses grow" gets deleted. "I help [specific type] get [specific result] in [timeframe]" gets read

## Example: Warm to Cold Transition

A freelance web designer:

**Month 1 (Warm):** Contact 500 personal connections. 15 conversations. 5 projects. Offer validated.

**Month 2 (Warm + Cold):** Continue warm follow-ups. Add cold email to 200 local businesses/week. 10 responses/week. 2 new projects/week.

**Month 3 (Cold + Content):** Cold email scaled to 500/week. Started posting 3x/week on LinkedIn. Content warms up cold leads who Google the designer's name before responding.

## Output

After reading this framework, you should be able to:
1. Decide whether to start with warm or cold outreach (almost always warm)
2. Know when to transition from warm to cold
3. Set appropriate volume targets for each type
4. Structure a hybrid approach that uses both effectively
