# Sales Conviction Builder

Diagnose which of the three conviction gaps is limiting your (or your team's) sales performance, then provide specific exercises to rebuild conviction from the ground up.

Read `references/frameworks/closer-framework-explained.md` for the sales framework that conviction supports. Read `references/frameworks/pricing-psychology.md` for how conviction relates to price presentation.

## Why This Matters

Hormozi identifies conviction as the #1 factor separating great salespeople from mediocre ones. It is not a soft skill. It is the foundation of everything else.

> "There are nine things that brilliant sales people do differently... and so if you boil it down a salesperson has three jobs to do: they have to maximize the number of opportunities they have, they have to convert the highest percentage of those opportunities, and - this is the important part - they have to do it consistently for a very long period of time."
> — Alex Hormozi, "Sales Was Hard Until I Understood These 9 Concepts"

Conviction is what allows someone to do all three jobs consistently. Without it, volume drops, close rates drop, and tenure drops. The salesperson burns out because they feel like they're pushing something they don't believe in.

The specific problem with price increases:

> "They're going to have an emotional comfort selling around a certain price. You have to accept that. There's a reason that you're the entrepreneur because you tend to be a little bit more adaptable."
> — Alex Hormozi, "How I RAISE PRICES without losing sales"

## The Three Conviction Gaps

### Gap 1: Product Conviction
**Symptom:** "I'm not sure our product actually delivers"
**Root cause:** The salesperson hasn't seen enough proof or doesn't understand the product deeply enough.

**Diagnostic questions:**
- Have you personally used the product/service?
- How many customer success stories can you tell from memory?
- When was the last time you talked to a happy customer?
- Can you explain exactly how the product creates the result?

**Exercises:**
1. **Customer immersion:** Talk to 5 successful customers this week. Record the conversations (with permission). Listen to them weekly.
2. **Product experience:** Use the product yourself. If it's a coaching program, go through it. If it's a service, shadow the delivery.
3. **Story bank:** Write down 10 specific customer results with names, numbers, and timelines. Memorize 3 as your go-to stories.
4. **Failure analysis:** For every customer who didn't get results, understand WHY. Was it the product or the customer? This builds nuanced conviction ("it works for people who do X").

### Gap 2: Price Conviction
**Symptom:** "The price feels too high" or "I wouldn't pay that much"
**Root cause:** The salesperson is comparing the price to their own financial reality instead of the value delivered.

**Diagnostic questions:**
- Would YOU pay this price for this result?
- What is the actual ROI for a customer who succeeds?
- What are customers currently spending on inferior alternatives?
- What is the cost of inaction for your ideal customer?

**Exercises:**
1. **ROI calculation:** Calculate the actual return on investment for 5 successful customers. Write it down: "Customer A paid $3K and made $45K." Read this daily.
2. **Alternative cost audit:** Research what prospects are spending on alternatives (courses, other services, DIY). Often they've already spent more than your price with worse results.
3. **The "home base" method:** If selling at a new higher price, use Hormozi's anchor-high method (see `/price-presentation-method`) so you're always "giving a discount."
4. **Luxury exposure:** Study premium products in other industries to normalize high prices. A Chanel bag is $8,000. A first-class flight is $15,000. Your $5,000 coaching program is affordable in context.

### Gap 3: Self-Conviction
**Symptom:** "Who am I to sell this?" or "I'm not a natural salesperson"
**Root cause:** Identity-level doubt about their right to sell, their competence, or their worthiness.

**Diagnostic questions:**
- Do you believe selling is helping or taking?
- Do you feel like you're "bothering" people on sales calls?
- When you close a deal, do you feel proud or guilty?
- Would you recommend your product to your best friend?

**Exercises:**
1. **Reframe selling:** Hormozi's frame: "If you truly believe your product helps people, it is your OBLIGATION to sell it. Not selling is withholding help."
2. **Win journal:** After every close, write down what happened and how you helped. After every loss, write down what you could have done better (not what's wrong with you).
3. **Volume therapy:** The cure for call reluctance is volume. Do 10 calls in a row with no break. By call 5, the anxiety is gone and you're in flow.
4. **Record and review:** Record your sales calls (with permission). Listen to yourself. The gap between how bad you think you sound and how you actually sound is almost always in your favor.

## The Consultation

### Step 1: Diagnose the Gap

Ask the user:

1. **Describe what happens when you try to sell.** (What do you feel? Where do you hesitate?)
2. **Which statement resonates most?**
   - "I'm not sure our product is good enough" → Product conviction
   - "The price feels too high" → Price conviction
   - "I feel uncomfortable selling" → Self-conviction
3. **What is your close rate?** (Compared to what it should be)
4. **Is this about you personally, or about your sales team?**

### Step 2: Assess the Evidence

For each gap type, gather evidence:

**Product:** "Tell me about your best 3 customer results."
**Price:** "What is the ROI for a successful customer?"
**Self:** "When was the last time you felt great after a sales call? What was different?"

### Step 3: Build the Action Plan

Based on the diagnosed gap, select 2-3 exercises from the relevant section above and build a weekly practice plan.
