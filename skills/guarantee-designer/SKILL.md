# Guarantee Designer

Walk through Hormozi's 4 guarantee types, diagnose which one fits your business model and risk tolerance, and produce exact guarantee language ready for your sales page or sales script.

Read `references/frameworks/guarantee-types-and-examples.md` for the full guarantee taxonomy with case studies.

## Why This Matters

Guarantees are the explicit mechanism for reducing perceived risk — the inverse of perceived likelihood. Most businesses either have no guarantee (leaving money on the table) or the wrong type of guarantee (creating refund problems).

Hormozi identifies four guarantee types, and the right one depends entirely on your business model:

> "There's four types of guarantees you can do: unconditional, conditional, zero guarantee, and then performance — what I call an implied guarantee... and then anti-guarantees where you lean into the fact that you don't have a guarantee."
> — Alex Hormozi, "How To Craft A $100M Offer In 6 Minutes"

The bold move Hormozi advocates, especially when starting out:

> "You almost want to make a guarantee that you're scared of. One, because you'll sell more people. Two, because if you're scared of it then you're going to work your ass off to try and fulfill on it."
> — Alex Hormozi, "How To Create A GRAND SLAM Offer"

## The Consultation

### Step 1: Assess the Business Context

Ask the user:

1. **What's your offer and price point?** (Higher price = more important to have a strong guarantee)
2. **What result do you deliver?** (Measurable result = conditional guarantee candidate. Subjective result = different approach.)
3. **What's your current refund rate?** (If >10%, the problem is delivery, not the guarantee.)
4. **What's your risk tolerance?** (Scale of 1-10: 1 = "I can't afford any refunds" / 10 = "I'll guarantee anything")
5. **Do any competitors offer a guarantee?** (If no one does, offering one is a massive differentiator.)
6. **How much control do you have over the customer's result?** (Done-for-you = high control. Coaching = shared control. Info products = low control.)

### Step 2: Walk Through the 4 Types

Present each type with its mechanics, risks, and ideal use cases:

#### Type 1: Unconditional Guarantee
**Mechanic:** Full refund, no questions asked, within a time window.
**Language:** "If you're not satisfied for any reason within [X days], we'll refund 100% of your investment."
**Best for:** Digital products, info products, low-ticket offers, businesses with strong delivery confidence.
**Risk:** Attracts tire-kickers at low price points. At high price points, self-selects for serious buyers (paradoxically lower refund rates).
**Hormozi's take:** This is the starting point for most businesses. If you can't offer this, ask yourself why — the answer usually reveals a delivery problem, not a guarantee problem.

#### Type 2: Conditional Guarantee
**Mechanic:** Refund or result guaranteed IF the customer meets specific conditions.
**Language:** "Do X, Y, and Z. If you don't get [result], we'll [refund / keep working for free / give bonus]."
**Best for:** Coaching, consulting, programs where customer effort determines outcome.
**Risk:** Must be able to verify conditions were met. Overly complex conditions feel like a scam.
**Key insight:** The conditions should be things that virtually guarantee the result if followed. This means the guarantee is really saying "follow the system and it works" — which is a statement of confidence, not exposure.

#### Type 3: Performance / Implied Guarantee
**Mechanic:** No explicit money-back language, but the structure implies guaranteed results.
**Language:** "We'll work with you until you hit [result]" or "If you don't make [X], I don't get paid."
**Best for:** Agencies, done-for-you services, high-ticket B2B, performance-based models.
**Risk:** Can extend delivery timelines. Must set boundaries on what "until" means.
**Hormozi example:** In the gym launch model, the performance was implied by the results data — "the average gym added $240K/year." The data was the guarantee.

#### Type 4: Anti-Guarantee
**Mechanic:** Explicitly lean into having NO guarantee as a positioning tool.
**Language:** "If you need a guarantee, this isn't for you. We work with people who are committed."
**Best for:** Premium/luxury positioning, audiences who self-select for commitment, when your results speak for themselves.
**Risk:** Only works if you have overwhelming social proof. Without proof, it just sounds arrogant.

> "Anti-guarantees — you lean into the fact that you don't have a guarantee. 'If this type of person needs a guarantee, this isn't for you.'"
> — Alex Hormozi, "How To Craft A $100M Offer In 6 Minutes"

### Step 3: Match to Business Model

Use this decision tree:

```
Do you control the outcome? (done-for-you)
├── YES → Performance Guarantee (Type 3) or Unconditional (Type 1)
└── NO → Does the customer's effort determine the result?
    ├── YES → Conditional Guarantee (Type 2)
    └── NO → Is your proof overwhelming?
        ├── YES → Anti-Guarantee (Type 4)
        └── NO → Unconditional Guarantee (Type 1) — build proof first
```

### Step 4: Write the Guarantee Script

Craft exact language for their sales context (sales page, sales call, proposal).

**Formula for guarantee language:**
1. Name the specific result
2. State the condition (if conditional)
3. State the remedy (refund, credit, extended service)
4. State the timeframe
5. Make it feel bold, not corporate
