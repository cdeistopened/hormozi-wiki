# Scarcity & Urgency Builder

Design legitimate scarcity (limited units) and urgency (limited time) mechanisms that shift the demand curve in your favor and create real pricing power. No fake countdown timers — structural mechanisms that are true, defensible, and profitable.

Read `references/frameworks/grand-slam-offer-anatomy.md` for how scarcity and urgency fit into the complete offer. Read `references/frameworks/value-equation-explained.md` for the underlying economics.

## The Economics

Hormozi learned this lesson at a Schwarzenegger charity event:

> "Last year we sold the tickets for $10,000 ahead just to attend the event and play poker. But this year it was $25,000 and we actually sold more people."
> — Alex Hormozi, "How To Charge Exorbitant Prices"

The gentleman who engineered this had made his fortune in ultra high-end jewelry. His principle:

> "You always have to remember — when demand increases, you cut supply."
> — Alex Hormozi, "How To Charge Exorbitant Prices"

This is not manipulation. This is microeconomics. When you cut supply while demand stays constant (or increases through marketing), prices rise and you can move further along the demand curve.

The Chanel model is the master class:

> "In a Chanel store, they have one to two bags available. Corporate doesn't let the stores know how many and of what bags they're going to receive — it's a complete mystery, complete surprise. And they don't tell them when they're going to stop producing stuff. Every bag is limited edition, there's only one of a kind, that might be the only bag you ever see."
> — Alex Hormozi, "How To Charge Exorbitant Prices"

## The Key Distinction

**Scarcity** = limited UNITS. "Only 5 spots available."
**Urgency** = limited TIME. "Price goes up Friday."

> "Scarcity is a function of units. Urgency is a function of time."
> — Alex Hormozi, "The $100M Offer Formula"

Both enhance the four core value variables. They don't change the offer itself, but they change the buying conditions.

> "I didn't change anything about the bottle itself, but it's significantly more valuable than if there's unlimited Gatorades."
> — Alex Hormozi, "The $100M Offer Formula"

## The Consultation

### Step 1: Assess Current Demand

Ask the user:
1. **What do you sell and at what price?**
2. **How many customers/clients can you serve at a time?** (This is your natural capacity constraint.)
3. **How many inquiries/leads do you get per month?**
4. **What's your current close rate?**
5. **Do you have a waitlist? If not, could you?**
6. **What's your sales channel?** (Sales calls, sales page, in-person, e-commerce)

### Step 2: Design Scarcity Mechanisms

Choose from legitimate scarcity levers:

| Mechanism | How It Works | Best For |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **Capacity cap** | "I only take 5 clients at a time, period." | Service businesses, consulting, coaching |
| **Cohort model** | "Next cohort starts April 1. 20 spots." | Courses, group programs, masterminds |
| **Limited edition** | "We only made 100 of these." | Physical products, digital products, events |
| **Seasonal availability** | "We only do this in Q1 and Q3." | Programs, launches, seasonal businesses |
| **Geographic scarcity** | "Only available in [market]." | Local businesses, regional services |

The crucial insight about capacity:

> "One of the interesting things about being at full capacity is it's completely up to you what full capacity means. You can say today that you are full."
> — Alex Hormozi, "How To Charge Exorbitant Prices"

### Step 3: Design Urgency Mechanisms

Choose from legitimate urgency levers:

| Mechanism | How It Works | Best For |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **Price increase announcement** | "Price goes up on [date]" | Any business raising prices — creates a natural pipeline clearing event |
| **Enrollment window** | "Applications close Friday" | Courses, coaching programs, cohort-based offers |
| **Bonus expiration** | "This bonus is only available this week" | Any offer with bonuses |
| **Waitlist pressure** | "We have 12 people on the waitlist" | Service businesses near capacity |
| **Event-driven deadline** | "The event is March 28 — register by the 25th" | Events, workshops, live programs |

Hormozi's specific process for using price increases as urgency:

> "Always, always, always clear the pipe. So clear the pipeline — announce it. Always own your price increases. It's one of the easiest ways to create true scarcity and urgency."
> — Alex Hormozi, "How I RAISE PRICES"

### Step 4: The Pressure-and-Release Rhythm

Hormozi describes managing demand like a pot of steam:

> "You cut your supply to zero, and then what happens is this pressure builds, like a pot. And then you want to periodically let off the steam. You don't want to be letting off the steam all the time because the pressure never builds. And that pressure is your pricing power."
> — Alex Hormozi, "How To Charge Exorbitant Prices"

Design a rhythm:
- **Closed period:** Build demand, collect waitlist, create content, nurture
- **Open period:** Limited window, cap enrollment, raise prices
- **Repeat:** Each cycle builds more demand pressure

### Step 5: The Overlooked Revenue

Most entrepreneurs underprice because they never learn what the top of the demand curve looks like:

> "If I were to sell a day of my time for five people at $20,000 each — sure. But what I'm missing is the number one and number two people of those five might have been willing to pay $200,000 for that day."
> — Alex Hormozi, "How To Charge Exorbitant Prices"

The fix: fewer units at higher prices, not more units at lower prices. Three clients at $100K generates the same revenue as five at $20K, with less operational drag and better delivery.
