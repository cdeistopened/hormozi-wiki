# Price Presentation Method

Design the exact language and choreography for presenting your price using Hormozi's anchor-above-target method with uneven splits and prepayment discounts.

Read `references/frameworks/pricing-psychology.md` for the full pricing psychology framework. Read `references/frameworks/closer-framework-explained.md` for how price presentation fits into the CLOSER flow.

## Why This Matters

How you present the price matters more than the price itself. Hormozi's method transforms a price increase into a perceived discount:

> "The way that I do it is I actually bump my top price up by an additional percent. And so I actually make the new price four thousand dollars, which is the price anchor. This is what the sales people are going to say on the phone."
> — Alex Hormozi, "How I RAISE PRICES without losing sales"

The system has four components working together:
1. **Home base** - the number your sales team is comfortable saying
2. **Price anchor** - a higher number that makes the target feel reasonable
3. **Prepayment discount** - the actual price you want, positioned as a deal
4. **Uneven split** - front-loaded payment that increases cash flow

> "It's four thousand dollars. You can either pay 2k today and then 2k in 30 days, or you can get same day savings and pay 3k today."
> — Alex Hormozi, "How I RAISE PRICES without losing sales"

## The Consultation

### Step 1: Gather Pricing Context

Ask the user:

1. **What is your current price?** (The number you're selling at today)
2. **What is your target price?** (Where you want to be)
3. **How is payment currently structured?** (One-time, payment plan, subscription)
4. **How does your sales team present the price now?** (Script or approach)
5. **What is the typical close rate?**

### Step 2: Design the Price Architecture

Apply Hormozi's formula:

| Component | Formula | Example ($2K → $3K) | Example ($4K → $5K) |
|-----------|---------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **Home Base** | Current price | $2,000 | $4,000 |
| **Price Anchor** | Target + 33% | $4,000 | $6,000 |
| **Payment Plan** | Anchor split unevenly | $2K + $2K (30 days) | $4K + $2K (30 days) |
| **Prepay Discount** | Target price | $3,000 (save $1K) | $5,000 (save $1K) |

The key insight: the sales team says the anchor price ($4,000) on the call. Then they offer the payment plan starting at home base ($2,000). Then they offer the prepay "discount" which is actually your target price ($3,000).

> "This person always said 2k. Well it's like, well shoot, I can either pay 2k now, 2k in a month or so, or just an extra thousand to get to the 3,000. See how much easier this is to sell."
> — Alex Hormozi, "How I RAISE PRICES without losing sales"

### Step 3: Script the Presentation

**The Price Reveal Script:**

```
"So the investment for [program/service name] is [$ANCHOR].

You can break that into [$HOME BASE] today and [$REMAINDER] in 30 days.

Or, if you want to take care of everything today, I can do [$TARGET PRICE] -
that saves you [$DIFFERENCE] and you don't have to worry about a second payment."
```

**Why this works:**
1. Sales team says home base amount first (comfort zone)
2. Anchor creates reference point that makes target feel like a deal
3. "Save" language makes the higher-than-old price feel like a discount
4. Uneven split front-loads cash flow for the business

### Step 4: Handle the Sales Team Transition

When raising prices, the sales team's conviction matters more than the prospect's wallet.

> "They're going to have an emotional comfort selling around a certain price. You have to accept that. There's a reason that you're the entrepreneur because you tend to be a little bit more adaptable, a little bit more flexible."
> — Alex Hormozi, "How I RAISE PRICES without losing sales"

**Conviction management:**
1. Give them home base as their "safety net" - they can always fall back to asking for this number
2. The anchor + discount structure means they're GIVING a discount, not ASKING for more money
3. Practice the script until the anchor number rolls off the tongue naturally
4. Track prepay vs. payment plan ratios (target: 40-60% prepay)

### Step 5: Design the Uneven Split

Hormozi prefers uneven splits (more upfront, less later) for three reasons:
1. Higher cash flow upfront to fund onboarding costs
2. The gap between upfront and prepay feels small ("just an extra $1K")
3. Reduces refund risk (they've committed more money upfront)

| Price Point | Suggested Split | Prepay Price |
|------------|----------------|-------------|
| $4K anchor | $2K + $2K | $3K prepay |
| $6K anchor | $4K + $2K | $5K prepay |
| $8K anchor | $5K + $3K | $6K prepay |
| $10K anchor | $6K + $4K | $8K prepay |
| $12K anchor | $6K + $3K + $3K | $10K prepay |

> "I prefer to have an uneven split because I'm going to have more cost of onboarding, I prefer to make more cash flow up front."
> — Alex Hormozi, "How I RAISE PRICES without losing sales"
